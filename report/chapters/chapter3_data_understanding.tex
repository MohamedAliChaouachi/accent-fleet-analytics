\chapter{Data Understanding through Source Profiling}
\label{chap:data}

\section{Introduction}
The previous chapter has defined the architectural target and the technology stack of the platform. The present chapter executes the second phase of the CRISP-DM methodology: the \emph{data understanding}. The chapter describes the three operational MySQL databases of \hostorg{} that supply the project, formalizes the dump-based extraction protocol used to bring their content into the analytical environment, presents the schema of the resulting staging layer through a data dictionary, audits the quality of the data with explicit SQL queries on PostgreSQL, profiles the missing values, and concludes with the exploratory data analysis that informs the feature engineering of Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}. All scripts referenced here are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:sql} and are available in the repository under \texttt{scripts/sql/} and \texttt{scripts/python/}.

\section{Source databases of \hostorg}

\subsection{Three operational MySQL databases}
As described in Chapter~\ref{chap:context}, the operational ecosystem of \hostorg{} relies on three MySQL databases that fulfil complementary roles. The first one --- denoted \emph{raw telemetry} --- collects the binary frames pushed by the GPS/IoT devices through SIM-card connectivity and persists them as \emph{hexadecimal} payloads. The second one --- denoted \emph{decoded archive} --- hosts the readable representation of the telemetry produced by an internal decoding toolchain; its tables follow the naming convention \texttt{arch\_xxxxx} and retain approximately the most recent six months of data. The third one --- denoted \emph{master data} --- carries the customers, drivers, vehicles, contracts and per-tenant configuration; its tables follow the convention \texttt{user\_xxxxx}, with one logical subgroup per tenant.

The present project consumes the second and third databases, namely the decoded \texttt{arch\_xxxxx} archive and the \texttt{user\_xxxxx} master data. The first database --- the raw hexadecimal stream --- is documented for completeness but is not exploited directly: its content has already been transformed by the internal decoding scripts of \hostorg{} before it enters the archive, and the project consumes the result of that transformation rather than re-implementing it.

\subsection{Tables of interest}
Within the two consumed databases, five families of tables have been retained for the analytical scope of the project. The trip-related family in the archive (\texttt{path}, \texttt{rep\_overspeed}, \texttt{stop}); the alert family (\texttt{notification}); the daily-aggregate family (\texttt{rep\_activity\_daily}); the high-resolution telemetry family in the archive (\texttt{archive}, with about 54.7~million rows over the available history); and the master-data family in the user database (\texttt{vehicule}, \texttt{driver}, \texttt{maintenance}, \texttt{document}, fueling tables). After conversion, these tables populate the staging schema of the analytical PostgreSQL with a faithful one-to-one correspondence.

\section{Acquisition strategy}

\subsection{Dump-based extraction from MySQL}
Because the operational databases run on MySQL and are not directly reachable from the analytical environment, the data is acquired through a dump-based protocol. The engineering team of \hostorg{} produces \texttt{.sql} dump files of the relevant slices of the \texttt{arch\_xxxxx} archive and of the \texttt{user\_xxxxx} master data, which are then transferred to the analytical environment. The dumps are converted to a PostgreSQL-compatible representation by the conversion bridge documented in Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}, and finally loaded into the staging schema. This extraction protocol guarantees that the operational MySQL workload is not perturbed by the analytical activity: the load on the production system is bounded to the duration of the dump itself, which is scheduled outside customer-facing peak hours.

\subsection{Connection to the analytical PostgreSQL}
Once the converted dump has been loaded, all subsequent profiling and exploration is performed directly on the analytical PostgreSQL. The connection from a developer notebook to the database follows the standard Python toolchain (\texttt{psycopg2} or SQLAlchemy), through an SSH tunnel terminating on \texttt{localhost:5432} of the Azure VM. The connection parameters are externalized in a \texttt{.env} file, with credentials of a read-only service account dedicated to analytical extraction. Listing~\ref{lst:psycopg-connect} shows the canonical connection snippet.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python,caption={Read-only connection from Python to the analytical PostgreSQL.},label={lst:psycopg-connect}]
import os
from sqlalchemy import create_engine

PG_DSN = (
    f"postgresql+psycopg2://{os.environ['PG_USER']}:{os.environ['PG_PWD']}"
    f"@{os.environ['PG_HOST']}:{os.environ['PG_PORT']}/{os.environ['PG_DB']}"
)
engine = create_engine(PG_DSN, pool_pre_ping=True, future=True)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{MCP-driven exploration}
During the data understanding phase, the Model Context Protocol (MCP) PostgreSQL server has been used as a programmable interface for ad-hoc exploration. Each exploratory question (row counts, null rates, distinct values, distributions) is expressed as an SQL query and submitted to the MCP server, which returns a structured result that is post-processed in the notebooks. This setup substantially accelerates the iteration cycle and standardizes the queries between the two members of the binomial.

\subsection{Reproducible export to CSV}
For reproducibility purposes, the principal exploratory queries are also exported as CSV through a dedicated Python script (\texttt{scripts/python/extract\_to\_csv.py}, Appendix~\ref{app:python}). The script reads a manifest of queries, executes them against the analytical PostgreSQL and writes the results to a versioned \texttt{exports/} directory, allowing the binomial to share intermediate datasets without re-running the whole pipeline.

\section{Schema analysis of the staging layer}

\subsection{Schema inspection queries}
Once the converted dumps have been loaded into the staging schema, the schema is inspected through the standard catalogue queries of PostgreSQL on \texttt{information\_schema}. Listing~\ref{lst:schema-columns} shows the canonical inspection query, which retrieves, for every column of every table, its name, its data type, its nullability, its default and its ordinal position.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=sql,caption={Schema inspection query against \texttt{information\_schema}.},label={lst:schema-columns}]
SELECT
    table_schema,
    table_name,
    column_name,
    data_type,
    is_nullable,
    column_default,
    ordinal_position
FROM information_schema.columns
WHERE table_schema IN ('staging', 'public')
ORDER BY table_schema, table_name, ordinal_position;
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Data quality assessment}

\subsection{Row count profile}
A first quality indicator is the row count per staging table, computed by Listing~\ref{lst:row-count}. The result of the query is stored as a baseline against which subsequent runs can be compared in order to detect any abnormal contraction or expansion of the staging layer between two dump cycles.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=sql,caption={Row counts per staging table of interest.},label={lst:row-count}]
SELECT 'path' AS table_name, COUNT(*) AS rows FROM staging.path
UNION ALL
SELECT 'rep_overspeed',         COUNT(*) FROM staging.rep_overspeed
UNION ALL
SELECT 'stop',                  COUNT(*) FROM staging.stop
UNION ALL
SELECT 'notification',          COUNT(*) FROM staging.notification
UNION ALL
SELECT 'rep_activity_daily',    COUNT(*) FROM staging.rep_activity_daily
UNION ALL
SELECT 'archive',               COUNT(*) FROM staging.archive
ORDER BY rows DESC;
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Null-rate analysis}
For every column of every table of interest, the null rate is computed by Listing~\ref{lst:null-rate}. The null rates are then post-processed into a heatmap rendered by the Python script \texttt{fig\_missing\_heatmap.py}, reproduced in Figure~\ref{fig:missing-heatmap}. Beyond their intrinsic descriptive value, the null rates also act as an indirect monitor of the conversion bridge: an unexpected drift of the null share for a column suggests an issue in the MySQL-to-PostgreSQL conversion of that column.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=sql,caption={Null-rate analysis for \texttt{staging.path}.},label={lst:null-rate}]
SELECT
    'distance_driven' AS column_name,
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN distance_driven IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) AS null_pct
FROM staging.path
UNION ALL
SELECT 'max_speed',
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN max_speed IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) FROM staging.path
UNION ALL
SELECT 'path_duration',
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN path_duration IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) FROM staging.path
UNION ALL
SELECT 'fuel_used',
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN fuel_used IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) FROM staging.path;
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Referential integrity}
The referential integrity between the staging tables of the archive and the master-data tables imported from the \texttt{user\_xxxxx} database is verified by Listing~\ref{lst:integrity}. The query identifies the devices appearing in the trip table that are not linked to any vehicle through the assignment chain, and quantifies their share in the total volume.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=sql,caption={Referential integrity between \texttt{staging.path} and the device--vehicle chain.},label={lst:integrity}]
SELECT COUNT(DISTINCT p.device_id) AS orphan_devices,
       SUM(CASE WHEN v.vehicule_id IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS orphan_rows
FROM staging.path p
LEFT JOIN staging.assignment a USING (device_id)
LEFT JOIN staging.vehicule  v ON v.vehicule_id = a.vehicule_id;
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Outlier detection}
Outliers on the physical bounds (negative distances, durations, speeds above 200~km/h) are detected by Listing~\ref{lst:outliers}. The result of this query motivates the cleaning rules \texttt{C2}, \texttt{C3} and \texttt{C5} formalized in Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=sql,caption={Detection of physically implausible values in \texttt{staging.path}.},label={lst:outliers}]
SELECT
    SUM(CASE WHEN path_duration <= 0 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS bad_duration,
    SUM(CASE WHEN distance_driven <= 0 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS bad_distance,
    SUM(CASE WHEN max_speed > 200 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS bad_speed
FROM staging.path;
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}

The distribution of every quantitative variable has been studied through descriptive statistics and through histograms generated by \texttt{fig\_distributions.py}. Figure~\ref{fig:dist-distance} shows, by way of example, the histogram of the per-trip distance, on a logarithmic scale, which exhibits a typical right-skew compatible with operational fleets in which short urban trips dominate the count and long inter-city trips dominate the total mileage.

\subsection{Bivariate analysis}
A bivariate analysis between the trip distance and the maximum speed shows a smooth growth of the maximum speed with the distance, with a saturation around 90~km/h on a clear majority of trips. A second analysis between the harsh-event ratio and the overspeed ratio reveals a separation of the operational regimes per tenant, anticipated in Chapter~\ref{chap:context}.

\subsection{Per-tenant signatures}
The aggregation of the principal indicators per tenant confirms two distinct regimes: tenant \texttt{264} exhibits an \emph{overspeed-rich} behavioural signature, with high overspeed-per-100~km values, while tenant \texttt{1787} exhibits a \emph{harsh-event-rich} signature. Tenants \texttt{235} and \texttt{238} occupy intermediate positions. This contrast directly informs the per-tenant fitting strategy retained in Chapter~\ref{chap:model}.

\subsection{Temporal coverage}
A monthly time series of trip counts and distances has been computed and plotted on the staging slice. The series shows a stable activity from 2025-01 onward, the period during which the archive-derived signals (harsh events, telemetry) are also available. The combination of the activity series and the archive coverage motivates the restriction of the modeling window to 2025-01--2026-04. Beyond that window, the six-month retention policy of the operational \texttt{arch\_xxxxx} tables would have constrained the depth of the historical archive.

\section{Synthesis of identified issues}
The data understanding phase has identified eleven principal data issues, listed in Table~\ref{tab:issues}. They are formalized as cleaning rules in Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Synthesis of the data quality issues detected on the staging tables.}
\label{tab:issues}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{8cm}|c|}
\hline
\textbf{ID} & \textbf{Issue} & \textbf{Source} \\
\hline
DQ-1 & Trip timestamps below 2019-10-01 (epoch artefacts of the legacy ingestion). & \texttt{path} \\
DQ-2 & Trip durations $\le 0$. & \texttt{path} \\
DQ-3 & Trip distances $\le 0$. & \texttt{path} \\
DQ-4 & Fuel values outside $[0,500]$~L. & \texttt{path} \\
DQ-5 & Maximum speed $>$ 200~km/h. & \texttt{path} \\
DQ-6 & Stop durations $\le 0$ or $>$ 1 year. & \texttt{stop} \\
DQ-7 & Devices not linked to any vehicle in the master data. & \texttt{path}, \texttt{vehicule} \\
DQ-8 & Archive rows with ignition $\neq$ 1 used for harsh events. & \texttt{archive} \\
DQ-9 & Accelerometer values outside the int8 range. & \texttt{archive} \\
DQ-10 & Archive speed outside $[0, 250]$. & \texttt{archive} \\
DQ-11 & Archive RPM outside $[0, 8000]$. & \texttt{archive} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\section{Conclusion}
This chapter has executed the data understanding phase by mobilizing the PostgreSQL profiling toolchain on the staging slice converted from the MySQL dumps of \hostorg. It has documented the role and the schema of the three operational databases of the company, characterized the dump-based extraction protocol, profiled the staging tables through dedicated SQL queries and conducted an exploratory data analysis on the principal indicators. The eleven data quality issues identified in this chapter become, in Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}, the input specification of the cleaning rule engine that opens the data preparation phase.
