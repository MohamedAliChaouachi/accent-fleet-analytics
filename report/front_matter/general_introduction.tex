\markboth{GENERAL INTRODUCTION}{GENERAL INTRODUCTION}

The growing affordability of GPS tracking devices, the maturity of cloud platforms and the widespread adoption of telematics solutions across logistics fleets have produced an environment in which raw vehicular data is no longer the bottleneck. The bottleneck has shifted toward the engineering of pipelines that can transform high-volume, high-velocity telemetry into reliable analytical artefacts and into actionable risk signals. Within this context, fleet operators face two parallel pressures: the need to reduce operational risks (accidents, fuel waste, maintenance costs) and the need to provide the operations team with explanatory dashboards that translate technical metrics into management decisions.

The host organization of this thesis, \hostorg, operates a multi-tenant telematics platform supporting hundreds of vehicles distributed across several customer fleets. The legacy analytical layer of the platform was a monolithic batch pipeline rebuilt every fifteen to forty-five minutes, which struggled to scale with the historical archive of fifty-four million telemetry pings and seven million trip records. Beyond the pure performance issue, the legacy pipeline did not offer a versioned model layer, did not separate cleanly the bronze, silver and gold layers of the warehouse and offered no machine-learning surface for behavioural analysis. The internship that this thesis documents was scoped explicitly to address these limitations.

The objective of the project is to design and to industrialize a CRISP-DM-aligned pipeline that ingests the raw telematics data from PostgreSQL, applies a transparent cleaning rule engine, materializes a dimensional warehouse and a family of analytical marts, trains an unsupervised behavioural segmentation and an anomaly-driven risk score per device-month, and finally exposes these artefacts through an API and a business intelligence dashboard, all hosted on Microsoft Azure infrastructure. A particular attention has been given to the operational concerns: incremental processing, idempotency, watermarking, monitoring, secure remote collaboration through SSH tunnelling and IP allowlisting on the Azure Virtual Machine.

The thesis is organized in six chapters. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:context}} situates the project in its industrial and academic context, presents the host organization, analyses the existing system and motivates the choice of CRISP-DM as the guiding methodology. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:business}} formalizes the business understanding, lists the research questions, derives the functional and non-functional requirements, and presents the target architecture and technology stack. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:data}} documents the data understanding phase, with PostgreSQL-driven schema inspection, data dictionary, missing-value analysis and exploratory data analysis. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:prep}} describes the data preparation pipeline, the cleaning rule engine and the engineering of the analytical features. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:model}} reports the modeling experiments, comparing baseline approaches, machine learning and deep learning, and justifies the final unsupervised choice. \textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:deploy}} presents the deployment of the pipeline and the models on Azure, the API and the dashboard, the cron-based automation, the monitoring layer and the secure remote operations setup. The general conclusion synthesizes the deliverables, the lessons learned and the perspectives opened by the project.
